The article body is here. This is a template — no fake article has been written. When a real piece is published, this is where the opening paragraph would sit, with a generous drop cap and a typeface that takes its time. The column is narrow on purpose: about seventy characters per line, the width that has been kind to readers since the first newspapers.

A second paragraph would follow, building the argument. The serif is Fraunces, chosen because it carries warmth without ornament. It looks at home in a match report, and at home in a long Saturday-morning piece.

A subhead, when the argument turns

Subheads break the piece into movements. They are not summaries; they are signposts. They give the reader permission to stop, pour another coffee, and come back.

A pull quote sits here when an argument deserves a moment of emphasis — and only then. We use them sparingly.

After the pull quote, the prose resumes. The whole point of a publication like this is that the writing is not in a hurry. We are not chasing a news cycle. We are trying to say something that will be worth reading next month, and the month after.

A closing

The piece ends without a flourish. It just ends. The last line is the one the reader carries away.
